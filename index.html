What is a flood?

  • Water submerging usually dry land
  • Occurs along rivers, lakes, sea shores or in flat areas saturated with water (e.g. after heavy rain)
  • Causes physical damage and affects water, food and crops supplies and supports spread of water-borne diseases

Water - Physical properties

  • Water is a molecule containing one oxygen (O) and two hydrogen (H) atoms
  • Chemical formula is H2O
  • H2O is a polar molecule
    • Center of negative charge near O atom
    • Center of positive charge near H atom
  • Therefore H2O acts as a dipole
    • Dipole = pair of two commensurate and opposite electric charges


Water molecule (Serway & Beichner, 2004)

Water – Orientational polarization

Polar H2O molecules are randomly oriented when no electric field is present




If an external electric field is applied, the H2O molecules partially align with the field; if external electric field is switched off, molecules swing back



Orientational polarization of polar molecules (Serway & Beichner, 2004)
  • If dipoles are orientational polarized:
    • They show strong interaction with electric field → high dielectric constant
    • Impinging wave is scattered back almost entirely
    • This is the case for water surfaces or very wet soils (large amount of water and thus high dielectric constant)
  • Only liquid water as an abundant natural material shows orientational polarization

Water - Orientational polarization

  • Orientational polarization depends on:
    • Temperature
      • In ice (frozen water) the H2O dipole are immobilized and therefore show no orientational polarization
    • Frequency of the alternating electric field
      • Above a certain frequency – the relaxation frequency – the dipoles cannot follow the oscillations of the electric field any longer
        • εr decreases
        • Microwave remote sensing of water/soil moisture is possible only below the relaxation frequency of water (≈10 GHz)!

Water - Dielectric properties

  • Dielectric constant of a material (e.g. soil) is directly related to its water content
  • Dielectric properties determine scattering and attenuation characteristics of a medium (attenuation = loss of energy of the wave)
  • These effects characterized by complex relative dielectric constant εr:

    • εr = ε'r - iε''r

    i = √-1

    ε'r real part, lossless dielectric constant

    ε''r imaginary part, describes the energy losses

  • Examples:
    • dry soil: εr ≈ 2-4
    • water: εr ≈ 73 (at 5 GHz [C-band, e.g. Sentinel-1] and 20°C)

Water - Dielectric properties

  • Effect of attenuation is exponential
    • Exponential decay of wave as it travels through a medium
  • Penetration depth δd:
    • Defined as distance where power of wave is reduced to 1/e
    • Related to ε'r and ε''r via the wavelength

      • δd = λ√ε'r / 2πε''r

    • λ ~ δd : the longer the wavelength λ the greater the penetration depth δd at a given dielectric constant
    • εr ~ 1/δd : the larger εr the smaller the penetration depth δd at given wavelength
  • Penetration depth into water surfaces is very small (in the range of a few millimeters)

Why use radar remote sensing?

Mapping water with microwave portion of the electromagnetic spectrum is one of the major application fields of radar remote sensing.

  • All weather capability - penetrates clouds
  • Independent of sun illumination - day and nigth operation
  • Sensitivity to dielectric properties - sensitivity to water content
  • Clear distiction between water surfaces and land


Copernicus Sentinel-1 maps floods from Idai (© contains modified Copernicus Sentinel data (2019), Cosmo-SkyMed, processed by GAF AG/e-GEOS/CMEMS, source).

Principles of imaging radar

  • Active remote sensing system - system transmits energy (pulses) and detects reflected responses (echos) from objects
  • Energy is tranmitted and recorded by radar antenna: Real Aperture Radar (RAR, airborne), Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR, satellite) systems
  • To distinguish between objects, systems operate in side looking geometry


Radar Imaging Geometry (© DLR, source)

Azimuth - along-track direction, parallel to the radar flight path

Range - across-track direction, perpendicular to the radar fligth path

Ground range - range direction projected onto the Earth`s reference plane

SAR backscatter from water

  • Mapping of water surfaces (waterbodies, wetlands, flooded areas) is based on different backscatter regimes of water surface and land surface.
    • Calm water surfaces appear smooth and cause specular reflection leading to low backscatter
    • Surrounding land surface appears much rougher causing higher backscatter

Difference in backscatter mechanisms for open water surfaces and dry land surfaces (after Solbø & Solheim, 2004)

SAR backscatter from flooded land

Changes in radar signal scattered back to the sensor from dry, wet and flooded soil (after Bourgeau-Chavez et al., 2009)

  • A to B: First backscatter increases with increasing soil moisture
  • C: then with increasing water level, backscatter becomes weaker with more and more specular reflection (scattering away from the sensor)
  • VV polarization sensitive to soil moisture and flood conditions

Flood mapping - methods

  • One simple and common method for floods mapping is thresholding.
    • Backscatter below threshold classified as flooded land
    • Backscatter above threshold classified as dry land
    • Thresholds derived from image histograms
    • Results in binary mask (0 = land, 1 = water)

      • Histogram of two Radarsat SAR images of the same region acquired under different incidence angles (Solbø & Solheim, 2004)

    • Contrast between land and open water surface increases with increasing incidence angle
